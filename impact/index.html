Impact

Selected impact case studies — how our research with new forms of urban big data shapes policy and practice.

Urban analytics is an applied field that brings the newest developments in data science and AI into the traditional urban planning domain. My aim has always been to positively influence policy and practice in using new forms of urban big data and advanced quantitative methods to understand urban phenomena — and to use credible, persuasive evidence to improve the form and sustainability of the built environment.

1 · Hot Homes — heat inequality and sensor-enhanced housing surveys (Southwark, London)

Partners: the Bureau of Investigative Journalism & Impact on Urban Health 2023–

In Hot Homes (August–September 2023), the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Impact on Urban Health and our UBDC team fitted sensors in 40 South London homes, recording temperature, humidity and air quality every minute, alongside qualitative accounts of residents’ health and wellbeing — developing and field-testing our sensor-enhanced housing survey methodology for monitoring indoor heat.

Headline findings:

  • Despite a relatively mild summer, every monitored home exceeded the WHO-recommended 25°C indoor maximum for London — one stayed above it for over three weeks, including at night
  • Some homes were 10°C hotter inside than outside overnight
  • During the one hot week, 80% of participants said the heat at home was affecting their physical and mental health

From data to policy — the Southwark influence chain:

  1. Autumn 2023 · consultation timing. The monitoring period coincided with Southwark’s public consultation on its draft Climate Resilience and Adaptation Strategy (29 Aug – 9 Oct 2023); the council worked with the research team to consider the data, and officers met study participants on 13 September 2023 to hear residents’ experiences first-hand
  2. Consultation report. The report includes a dedicated “Hot homes study” section, and “hot homes” was listed among the key issues raised in the Cabinet report
  3. February 2024 · strategy adopted. The Climate Resilience and Adaptation Strategy introduced Action A7 — “Develop an approach to tackling ‘hot homes’ so residents who are most vulnerable to this issue have cooler homes” (jointly led by Asset Management and Public Health) — alongside doubling cool spaces by 2026, home-temperature monitoring via social care visits, overheating advice through the Green Homes Advice Service, and climate risk mapping, with £1m allocated
  4. September 2024 · annual report. Southwark’s Climate Change Annual Report explicitly credits the Bureau’s “landmark study” as the driver of the hot homes action
  5. Spring 2025 · exemplar practice. The LGA / London Councils London Leading series features Southwark’s strategy development, noting the SIIP (2024–27) data programme supporting the hot-homes pilot
  6. May 2025 · public health evidence. The Hot Weather & Health JSNA uses Hot Homes as core evidence — 1 in 8 participants sought medical care as a direct result of their hot home; every participating household with a baby exceeded temperature thresholds linked to sudden infant death risk; communal-heating homes ran about 1°C hotter; and 25% of participants had “nowhere to go” for respite — justifying the expansion of cool spaces
  7. September 2025 · strategies merged. Cabinet merged the 2021 Climate Change Strategy and the 2024 adaptation strategy so that adaptation carries equal weight with emissions reduction; the Hot Homes lineage continues in Action 1 (a portfolio of measures for residents most vulnerable to overheating, embedded in housing asset management) and Action 24 (an NHS/voluntary-sector programme built on the JSNA recommendations, plus expanded cool spaces coverage)

  • Southwark Climate Resilience and Adaptation Strategy — adopted February 2024, with the "hot homes" Action A7 (PDF)
  • Southwark Climate Change Strategy & Action Plan Update — Cabinet report, 16 September 2025 (merged strategy)
  • Southwark Hot Weather & Health JSNA — May 2025, via the Southwark Insight Hub

Continuing relevance (2026):

  • Heat Ready London (June 2026) — London’s first heat plan, with 37 priority areas across six sectors and around 1 million London homes at high risk of overheating
  • The National Heat Risk Commission (established March 2026, chaired by Emma Howard Boyd at the LSE Grantham Research Institute) — interim report summer 2026, final report 2027
  • The Climate Change Committee’s A Well-Adapted UK (2026) names heatwaves the UK’s greatest climate risk to health
  • The London Assembly investigation “Are London’s homes ready for a heatwave?” — report expected later in 2026
  • Impact on Urban Health continues citing the study in heatwave advocacy
  • Lambeth Cooling Fiveways carries the heat-risk work into 2026, and peer-reviewed papers are now being published using the 2023 Southwark sensor dataset

Media and data: TBIJ (Jun 2023, Sep 2023, Dec 2023, resident stories), the Mirror, University of Glasgow news, and an open dataset released via UBDC.

2 · Mobile phone app data for understanding travel behaviour — Glasgow City Council & Glasgow City Region

Partners: Glasgow City Council (GCC) & Glasgow City Region (GCR) 2020–

As project co-lead of UBDC’s mobile phone data research group, I helped turn two major mobile phone app datasets (Huq and Tamoco) into policy-ready mobility products — origin–destination (OD) matrices, home location estimation, and site visitation patterns — delivered to GCC and GCR for transport planning, greenspace management, and post-pandemic economic recovery, when traditional surveys were too slow to capture changing behaviour (methodology paper).

Where the products have been used:

  • Strategic Parking Plan — Atkins used the OD matrices to model travel and parking demand, feeding into the Glasgow Transport Strategy Spatial Delivery Framework
  • Clyde Mission — GCR analysed pre-/during-pandemic commuting to the Clyde River corridor
  • Open space use — visitation patterns for 51 open spaces; in-depth analyses of Kelvingrove Park and Glasgow Green (visitor volumes, local vs non-local, daily profiles) supporting Parks Management budgeting and the Open Space Strategy
  • Transport strategy appraisal — Jacobs/Steer combined the OD matrices with Census travel-to-work data to appraise interventions such as public transport corridors
  • Strategic Bus Network Plan — Jacobs used the OD matrices to help design the bus network Glasgow needs
  • City Network modelling — AECOM is using the OD matrices for full-city traffic modelling for the Liveable Neighbourhoods programme, with outputs owned by GCC for reuse
📄 Policy documents
  • Glasgow Transport Strategy — Spatial Delivery Framework — approved by the council's City Administration Committee, 8 February 2024 (PDF)
  • Glasgow Open Space Strategy — visitation evidence supporting the strategy's next steps and future evidence-based policies

Project pages and open data: the UBDC project Understanding the use of urban greenspaces with big data (in partnership with Glasgow City Council), the open Mobility Metrics for Glasgow City Region and Huq packaged data 2019–2024 datasets, the UBDC transport and mobility data catalogue, and joint outputs with GCC on greenspace visitation (GIScience 2021; GIScience 2023 poster — “A Collaboration with Glasgow City Council to Aid in Open Space Policy and Operations”).

In this work I deliberately enabled early-career colleagues to lead the partner relationships: PDRA Dr Michael Sinclair is now a permanent Lecturer in Urban Analytics at Glasgow, and PDRA Dr Saeed Maadi moved to a data science leadership role in local government.

3 · Data science with charities and the third sector — the Salvation Army & Sustrans

Partners: the Salvation Army & Sustrans 2017–

The Salvation Army (2017–). In my postdoctoral research in Arizona I optimised the Salvation Army Metro Phoenix utility assistance programme’s in-person interview allocation; the results were implemented in their Phoenix office and published in Applied Geography. At Glasgow I brought the model to the Salvation Army UK: we co-designed an SGSSS collaborative PhD studentship (won in 2021, PhD student Mohd Sarim) analysing internal data from their Employment Plus and community services to improve service efficiency — with a longer-term aim of extending data-driven decision-making across the Salvation Army’s international branches and improving their data collection strategies.

Sustrans (2021–). Through an MSc collaborative dissertation (2021) and an MSc group project (2022) on primary and secondary school street interventions, our analyses were incorporated into Sustrans’ work — including the active travel dashboard showing how pupils travel to school. Sustrans provided a paid internship for the dissertation student, and one of our 2022–23 MSc Urban Analytics graduates now holds a permanent position there.

📄 Policy & practice outputs
  • Interview-allocation strategy implemented in the Salvation Army Metro Phoenix utility assistance programme (Applied Geography, 2020)
  • Analytical results informing internal service decisions in the Salvation Army UK's Employment Plus programme
  • School travel analyses incorporated into Sustrans' active travel dashboard for Scottish school streets

Emerging impact

  • Port digital twin with Peel Ports (Clydeport) — collaboration on digital twinning for the Clyde ports, supported by ESRC IAA and University of Glasgow Creative and Social Futures funding (case study in development)
  • Larger-scale indoor heat and energy monitoring — follow-on from the Southwark work with the Understanding Society Innovation Panel and the UCL Smart Energy Research Lab, informing net-zero and retrofitting policy evidence (case study in development)
