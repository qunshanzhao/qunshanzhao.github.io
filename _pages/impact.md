Urban analytics is an applied field that brings the newest developments in data science and AI into the traditional urban planning domain. My aim has always been to positively influence policy and practice in using new forms of urban big data and advanced quantitative methods to understand urban phenomena — and to use credible, persuasive evidence to improve the form and sustainability of the built environment.

<h2 id="heat" style="margin-top:2.2rem;scroll-margin-top:90px;">1 · Hot Homes — heat inequality and sensor-enhanced housing surveys (Southwark, London)</h2>

<p style="margin:4px 0 10px;">
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;font-weight:600;background:rgba(224,49,49,0.14);color:#e03131;">Partners: the Bureau of Investigative Journalism &amp; Impact on Urban Health</span>
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;border:1px solid rgba(128,128,128,0.4);">2023–</span>
</p>

In **[Hot Homes](https://lambethclimatepartnership.org/case-study/hot-homes)** (August–September 2023), the Bureau of Investigative Journalism, Impact on Urban Health and our UBDC team fitted sensors in 40 South London homes, recording temperature, humidity and air quality every minute, alongside qualitative accounts of residents' health and wellbeing — developing and field-testing our **sensor-enhanced housing survey** methodology for monitoring indoor heat.

Headline findings:

- Despite a relatively mild summer, **every monitored home** exceeded the WHO-recommended 25°C indoor maximum for London — one stayed above it for over three weeks, including at night
- Some homes were **10°C hotter inside than outside** overnight
- During the one hot week, **80% of participants** said the heat at home was affecting their physical and mental health

**From data to policy — the Southwark influence chain:**

1. **Autumn 2023 · consultation timing.** The monitoring period coincided with Southwark's public consultation on its draft Climate Resilience and Adaptation Strategy (29 Aug – 9 Oct 2023); the council worked with the research team to consider the data, and officers met study participants on 13 September 2023 to hear residents' experiences first-hand
2. **Consultation report.** The report includes a dedicated "Hot homes study" section, and "hot homes" was listed among the key issues raised in the Cabinet report
3. **February 2024 · strategy adopted.** The Climate Resilience and Adaptation Strategy introduced **Action A7** — *"Develop an approach to tackling 'hot homes' so residents who are most vulnerable to this issue have cooler homes"* (jointly led by Asset Management and Public Health) — alongside doubling cool spaces by 2026, home-temperature monitoring via social care visits, overheating advice through the Green Homes Advice Service, and climate risk mapping, with **£1m allocated**
4. **September 2024 · annual report.** Southwark's Climate Change Annual Report explicitly credits the Bureau's *"landmark study"* as the driver of the hot homes action
5. **Spring 2025 · exemplar practice.** The LGA / London Councils *London Leading* series features Southwark's strategy development, noting the SIIP (2024–27) data programme supporting the hot-homes pilot
6. **May 2025 · public health evidence.** The Hot Weather &amp; Health JSNA uses Hot Homes as core evidence — 1 in 8 participants sought medical care as a direct result of their hot home; every participating household with a baby exceeded temperature thresholds linked to sudden infant death risk; communal-heating homes ran about 1°C hotter; and 25% of participants had *"nowhere to go"* for respite — justifying the expansion of cool spaces
7. **September 2025 · strategies merged.** Cabinet merged the 2021 Climate Change Strategy and the 2024 adaptation strategy so that adaptation carries equal weight with emissions reduction; the Hot Homes lineage continues in **Action 1** (a portfolio of measures for residents most vulnerable to overheating, embedded in housing asset management) and **Action 24** (an NHS/voluntary-sector programme built on the JSNA recommendations, plus expanded cool spaces coverage)

**Continuing relevance (2026):**

- **[Heat Ready London](https://www.london.gov.uk/programmes-strategies/environment-and-climate-change/climate-change/climate-adaptation/heat-risk/heat-ready-london)** (June 2026) — London's first heat plan, with 37 priority areas across six sectors and around 1 million London homes at high risk of overheating
- The **[National Heat Risk Commission](https://www.lse.ac.uk/granthaminstitute/news/new-national-heat-risk-commission-announced/)** (established March 2026, chaired by Emma Howard Boyd at the LSE Grantham Research Institute) — interim report summer 2026, final report 2027
- The Climate Change Committee's **[A Well-Adapted UK](https://www.theccc.org.uk/publication/a-well-adapted-uk/)** (2026) names heatwaves the UK's greatest climate risk to health
- The **[London Assembly investigation](https://www.london.gov.uk/who-we-are/what-london-assembly-does/london-assembly-work/london-assembly-current-investigations/are-londons-homes-ready-heatwave)** *"Are London's homes ready for a heatwave?"* — report expected later in 2026
- **[Impact on Urban Health](https://urbanhealth.org.uk/insights/news/overheating-homes-climate-health-crisis)** continues citing the study in heatwave advocacy
- **[Lambeth Cooling Fiveways](https://lambethclimatepartnership.org/case-study/cooling-fiveways)** carries the heat-risk work into 2026, and peer-reviewed papers are now being published using the 2023 Southwark sensor dataset

<h2 id="mobility" style="margin-top:2.4rem;scroll-margin-top:90px;">2 · Mobile phone app data for understanding travel behaviour — Glasgow City Council &amp; Glasgow City Region</h2>

<p style="margin:4px 0 10px;">
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;font-weight:600;background:rgba(25,113,194,0.14);color:#1971c2;">Partners: Glasgow City Council (GCC) &amp; Glasgow City Region (GCR)</span>
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;border:1px solid rgba(128,128,128,0.4);">2020–</span>
</p>

As project co-lead of UBDC's mobile phone data research group, I helped turn two major mobile phone app datasets (Huq and Tamoco) into policy-ready mobility products — origin–destination (OD) matrices, home location estimation, and site visitation patterns — delivered to GCC and GCR for transport planning, greenspace management, and post-pandemic economic recovery, when traditional surveys were too slow to capture changing behaviour ([methodology paper](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apgeog.2023.102997)).

Where the products have been used:

- **Strategic Parking Plan** — Atkins used the OD matrices to model travel and parking demand, feeding into the Glasgow Transport Strategy Spatial Delivery Framework
- **Clyde Mission** — GCR analysed pre-/during-pandemic commuting to the Clyde River corridor
- **Open space use** — visitation patterns for 51 open spaces; in-depth analyses of Kelvingrove Park and Glasgow Green (visitor volumes, local vs non-local, daily profiles) supporting Parks Management budgeting and the Open Space Strategy
- **Transport strategy appraisal** — Jacobs/Steer combined the OD matrices with Census travel-to-work data to appraise interventions such as public transport corridors
- **Strategic Bus Network Plan** — Jacobs used the OD matrices to help design the bus network Glasgow needs
- **City Network modelling** — AECOM is using the OD matrices for full-city traffic modelling for the Liveable Neighbourhoods programme, with outputs owned by GCC for reuse

<h2 id="charity" style="margin-top:2.4rem;scroll-margin-top:90px;">3 · Data science with charities and the third sector — the Salvation Army &amp; Sustrans</h2>

<p style="margin:4px 0 10px;">
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;font-weight:600;background:rgba(47,158,68,0.16);color:#2f9e44;">Partners: the Salvation Army &amp; Sustrans</span>
  <span style="display:inline-block;padding:2px 10px;border-radius:1rem;font-size:0.8em;border:1px solid rgba(128,128,128,0.4);">2017–</span>
</p>

**The Salvation Army (2017–).** In my postdoctoral research in Arizona I optimised the Salvation Army Metro Phoenix utility assistance programme's in-person interview allocation; the results were implemented in their Phoenix office and published in [Applied Geography](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apgeog.2020.102241). At Glasgow I brought the model to the Salvation Army UK: we co-designed an SGSSS collaborative PhD studentship (won in 2021, PhD student [Mohd Sarim]({{ '/people/#mohd-sarim' | relative_url }})) analysing internal data from their Employment Plus and community services to improve service efficiency — with a longer-term aim of extending data-driven decision-making across the Salvation Army's international branches and improving their data collection strategies.

**Sustrans (2021–).** Through an MSc collaborative dissertation (2021) and an MSc group project (2022) on primary and secondary school street interventions, our analyses were incorporated into Sustrans' work — including the active travel dashboard showing how pupils travel to school. Sustrans provided a paid internship for the dissertation student, and one of our 2022–23 MSc Urban Analytics graduates now holds a permanent position there.
