Gaokao results are out and application season has begun; with a relative in the family taking the exam this year, I did a little research. It turns out that even after more than a decade, choosing a university programme is still a headache. Educational resources remain glaringly unequal across provinces and cities, and competition for the top universities has not eased one bit.

People now live longer and longer, and what matters most is the capacity for self-directed learning — adapting quickly to a changing society. The constraints of one's undergraduate major matter less and less. Still, some things are learnt faster and more efficiently when young: mathematics, languages, programming, and the like. Choosing a major matters, but choosing one does not lock you in. If you have no particular passion for any field, it is worth picking a major from which switching tracks is relatively easy. In STEM, such majors typically build solid foundations in mathematics, physics, and computing; with those foundations, the range of future options stays wide. In my own experience, I have moved through four distinct but interconnected fields since undergraduate — remote sensing/photogrammetry, geographic information science, industrial engineering, and urban planning — and what they share is precisely mathematics, physics, and computing; the difference lies only in how you apply the techniques and what problems you solve. Majors, in a sense, exist for the administrative convenience of universities; companies running projects simply need people who can solve problems — and will hire them from any background.

It must be acknowledged that different majors — and the industries behind them — differ enormously in competitiveness. At home or abroad, medicine, law, and business pay well (China's healthcare system is a special case, though I suspect things will converge eventually), and computing has minted countless fortunes in recent years, drawing enormous crowds. When choosing, consider whether you would rather be an ordinary person in a hot field, or a top person in a slightly less fashionable field (including the humanities and social sciences) that genuinely interests you — resources always tilt towards the top people, whatever the industry. It is also worth watching decadal technology forecasts: when a disruptive technology emerges in an industry, it delivers outsized premiums to that industry's practitioners in short order.

Many posts debate whether the university matters more, or the major, or the city. I would only say: adults do not make choices — they make trade-offs. When your score falls short, the question becomes which part to sacrifice, given your needs and family circumstances — and this ties directly to your plans after the degree. If you intend to pursue graduate study abroad or at home, a top programme at a relatively good university carries decisive weight, and the city hardly matters — you will move anyway. The city's influence is more about horizons and environment. These days every major city is competing to attract young workers; you do not have to study in a megacity.

From what I have observed in the US and the UK, overseas universities still favour applicants whose undergraduate degrees come from the former 985/211 — now "Double First-Class" — universities. It is a simple screening heuristic for wading through vast application piles, and a rough gauge of a Chinese student's quality and ability. In that respect the gaokao system retains real efficiency and advantages. That said, foreign universities are not rigid: some of China's newer universities are rising fast — ShanghaiTech, SUSTech, Shenzhen, Soochow — as are the Sino-foreign joint ventures such as UNNC, XJTLU, NYU Shanghai, and Duke Kunshan, and graduates of these schools do not necessarily lose out on brand.

The gaokao is one of life's important choices, but it is emphatically only a beginning; many more chances to choose lie ahead. Around me, plenty of young professors at overseas universities, and plenty of leaders in business and government, did their undergraduate degrees at all manner of schools — not the 985s people imagine. In the end, who goes furthest depends on how high you aim, how long you persist, and how much you love what you do. The four or five undergraduate years are the freest, most energetic, most possibility-filled years of a life — I hope everyone leaves themselves beautiful memories worthy of their youth.

Finally — a small advertisement for Remote Sensing at Wuhan University? The programme has alumni all over the world, decent room to grow, is not too exhausting, is not especially hard to learn, and offers plenty of opportunities to change tracks. Worth considering?
