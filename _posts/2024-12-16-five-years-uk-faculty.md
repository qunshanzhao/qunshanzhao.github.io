In the blink of an eye it is the end of 2024 — by January 2025, the time I have lived in the UK will equal the time I lived in the US. Time really does fly.

Looking back, this has been an extremely busy year for both work and life. I was very fortunate to be promoted to full Professor of Urban Analytics at Glasgow this year, so I want to take stock of the past five years — partly to leave a record for myself, and partly as a reference for friends interested in working in UK or European academia. With Trump back for another four years in the US, isolationism resurgent, and constant friction in China–US relations, I suspect many people are now looking at opportunities all over the world.

When I moved from the US to the UK five and a half years ago I did not think about it too deeply, but after arriving I discovered that Europe really is a rather different continent. Both countries speak English, Britain and America share some ancestry, and American companies love opening branches in — and buying up — the UK; yet the overall way society is organised, and the mindset, are quite different. If you have spent a long time in China or the US, you develop an instinctive "big country" mentality: huge markets, huge scale, competing in every field imaginable. In Europe, you realise each country is only so big; Britain has left the EU, and the EU itself is not an especially united entity. I used to joke with friends that if you treated Europe as a single whole, it might just about compete with China and the US on food, travel, weather, and a few other fronts. But when I threw that question at a table of continental colleagues in an Armenian restaurant during a conference in Manchester this year, their faces said it all. The conversation that followed was: "How could we European countries possibly be one whole? Not in several lifetimes."

Fair enough — perhaps a somewhat fragmented Europe is no bad thing, since it preserves the best cultural diversity and distinctiveness in the world.

Back to the UK: this is a country of nearly seventy million people, the great majority in England (fifty-odd million). Scotland is about half the size of England but has only a bit over five million people — a tenth of the population. The whole UK is roughly the size of Michigan, and ten US states are bigger than Michigan — never mind the joke on Xiaohongshu that Britain is the size of Shaanxi province. Industrially, the UK is dominated by finance; in IT there are branches of the big American firms (Google DeepMind, for example) and some smaller companies, plus biopharma, energy, chemicals, and some high-end manufacturing still running. So finding a job in the UK after graduating really is not that easy: the country is only so big, and there are only so many industries and openings — you cannot job-hop and relocate across an entire continent the way you can in the US or China. Europeans can work anywhere in the EU and have a language advantage, so they fare much better; for international students, this genuinely is a disadvantage compared with the two big countries.

For academic jobs in Europe, English gives the UK and Ireland a natural advantage in absorbing immigrant academics. On the continent, the Netherlands should be an excellent choice — English is almost universal — though in recent years it has been growing more conservative, with increasing demands for teaching in Dutch. Other major countries such as Germany, France, Switzerland, Austria, Belgium, and the Nordics have very good universities, but usually require you to teach in the local language after 3–5 years. If you are still young, learning more languages never hurts — or failing that, push the next generation to. A colleague in the office next door is fluent in English, German, Spanish, and Portuguese, and glides through half of Europe with ease. Beyond the language advantage, the UK academic system is also relatively friendly to early-career staff — many continental systems still revolve around one professor per department.

The UK position comparable to a tenure-track assistant professorship in the US, Hong Kong, or Singapore is the **Research & Teaching (R&T) Track Lecturer**. Canada has largely reformed its system along American lines, while many universities in Australia and New Zealand still follow Commonwealth conventions. UK universities also frequently hire teaching-focused positions on the **Learning & Teaching Track** — identical titles, but primarily teaching. Most R&T posts, and some L&T posts, are advertised as **permanent positions** — you can roughly think of it as being tenured on arrival: as long as the university is not in financial crisis, the job is permanent and relatively stable. If you can land an R&T Lectureship at a UK Russell Group university or a top school in your field, it is absolutely worth considering. The UK also has research-only positions, from research assistant and postdoctoral researcher up to senior researcher — and at some universities, professorial research fellows. Research-only contracts are not permanent, but they are promotable just like the other two tracks. I am not sure whether senior researchers in the US can convert directly to tenured professorships, but in the UK there certainly are cases of senior researchers becoming professors. Overall, the UK system is more inclusive, and in some respects more humane.

The best part of a UK academic job is the absence of tenure-track pressure. The assessment pressure is comparatively low, and you can start doing research you actually find interesting from day one. New staff usually have a probation period of six months to at most four years (depending on the university), but I have almost never heard of anyone failing it. At Glasgow, assessment is done within your own discipline, benchmarked against the other 23 Russell Group universities. There are seven main criteria: publications, research funding, PhD supervision, teaching, leadership, societal impact, and esteem within the field. For publications the emphasis is squarely on quality — neither journal tiers nor impact factors count; it all comes down to peer review in the national research assessment (a complete enigma). Research funding is fiercely competitive everywhere these days, but there are plenty of doors to knock on: UKRI, the EU's Horizon Europe, the European Space Agency, UK and global charities such as Wellcome, Leverhulme, and the Royal Society, and even industry and government contracts. The teaching load is generally not heavy — around two courses for new staff. And the UK evaluation system's strong emphasis on societal impact has, in a sense, brought academic research closer to society.

Of course, UK academia has its downsides. First, early-career salaries are considerably lower than at US R1/R2 universities or in Hong Kong and Singapore, and hold no real advantage over Canada, Australia, or New Zealand. Since the 2008 financial crisis Europe's growth has essentially stagnated, and the annual UK-wide university pay negotiations consistently fail to keep up with inflation (classic European egalitarianism); losing 1–2% a year compounds to roughly 20–30% underpayment over a decade or more — to say nothing of Europe's generally high-tax welfare systems and the depreciation of the pound. The relatively better-paying UK institutions are mostly in London — Imperial, LSE, London Business School — but across Europe everyone is much of a muchness, with Switzerland the one high-salary, low-tax exception. Second, new staff get essentially no start-up package — a token amount at best — so the start is slower. The logic is understandable: you are hired into a permanent job, so unlike a tenure-track system that wants a clear return on a new hire, the up-front investment is smaller. Third, the PhD system: British PhDs are short, with funding typically only 3–4 years (UK government 3–3.5 years; CSC 4 years), which leaves little room for error — if the first year or two go badly you can extend by six months to a year, but once the funding runs out, finances get difficult. And because the PhD is short, graduates generally need a postdoc or a postdoctoral fellowship to accumulate a track record — it is hard to land a faculty job straight out of a PhD. At most 30% of UK government PhD studentships can go to international students, yet domestic students are not particularly keen on doing PhDs — so competition for the international quota is ferocious while the domestic 70% goes unfilled. In our centre's urban big data area, I cannot recall recruiting a single British PhD student in the past five years. Fourth, research funding: the UK strongly emphasises interdisciplinarity — most medium-to-large grants demand cross-disciplinary collaboration and proactive network-building — and it is comparatively hard to find funding purely dedicated to basic research like the US NSF's. Finally, UK universities have lately fallen into financial crisis as government restrictions on master's students' dependant visas cut international enrolment, prompting belt-tightening and job cuts everywhere — though thanks to the unions, most universities have relied on voluntary severance schemes or hiring freezes.

The empire's sun set long ago, but its cultural influence is everywhere: UK academics routinely move to other Commonwealth countries, Hong Kong and Singapore, and the US (US faculty jobs generally require a US PhD or one from a top European school, otherwise a postdoc or prior US experience — though stars can go wherever they like). Because of Brexit, people can no longer move freely between the continent and Britain, but the Global Talent Visa is very friendly to researchers, especially postdocs — permanent residency after three years, and UK permanent residency carries none of the tax and residence constraints of a US green card. Housing is not cheap relative to income, but in general you can enjoy a rather good urban life.

Now for a few reflections on my own past five years, and the road ahead:

- Our **MSc in Urban Analytics** grew from nothing, with scholarships every year to support Scottish students, and an exchange programme with Aalto University in Finland now in its second year. We will keep expanding links with continental Europe, hopefully towards more joint master's and doctoral training.
- I did a good deal of research on **fundamental urban data** — its production, processing, distribution, and extension. It has really sunk in that data will remain the core asset: LLM code and weights can be open-sourced, but genuinely open training data is rare.
- I built up an **urban sensing** direction at the centre ([UBDC Urban Sensing](https://www.ubdc.ac.uk/urban-sensing/sensor-enhanced-housing-surveys)) with a miscellany of sensing projects. Going forward this will connect to urban sustainability and low-carbon/net-zero work, mainly in transport (air quality in Low Emission Zones) and housing (building energy efficiency models from local sensing).
- The most fun of these was our **sensor-enhanced housing survey** — buying sensors and 3D-printing enclosures was a delight. The interdisciplinary **multilingual cities** project on new geographies of language was fascinating too.
- During the pandemic I ran several short **rapid-response** projects, mostly analysing mobile phone app data, which taught me a great deal about delivering projects.
- With friends and colleagues I organised **Google Summer of Code** for three years, building up a solid open-source Python package for spatial optimisation ([PySAL/spopt](https://github.com/pysal/spopt)), mainly the facility location modelling part. More updates to come.
- I learnt to work with the **UK media**: our 2023 sensor project was covered by the Mirror ("UK facing summer health crisis as half of UK homes hit 'sauna-like' temperatures") and many other outlets, and the final research report informed policy at London's Southwark Council. In 2024 our Glasgow Low Emission Zone paper led to a BBC Radio Scotland interview and plenty of local coverage. I felt the power of the media — and became more careful about how information gets conveyed.
- I helped organise conferences big and small: GIScience 2023, CPGIS 2023, GISRUK 2023, AGILE 2024, the Turing Urban Analytics Event 2024, Data-driven Decarbonisation in Scotland, and more. Do come visit Glasgow — even if our weather really is nothing to write home about.
- The Gates Foundation-funded work on informal settlements in Nigeria and Kenya opened up our engagement with Africa ([the IDEAMAPS Network](https://www.ideamapsnetwork.org/)).
- My first PhD student, Niantang, graduated smoothly — all the best for the future!

For me personally, five years is neither long nor short — and three of them were pandemic years. I have come to believe the most important qualities for research are intrinsic **interest** and **resilience/discipline**: rejections of papers, and especially of grant proposals, are simply routine, and without genuine interest and toughness it is hard to keep going. Being a bit smarter on top of that is a bonus. Time management matters enormously — watch fewer short videos. In the next 3–5 years I hope to keep doing more interesting research and to further strengthen our undergraduate, master's, and doctoral training. My thanks to everyone who has helped me over these years, and to the postdocs, PhD students, and master's students who work alongside me. One person's strength is always limited; a group of people is another matter entirely.
